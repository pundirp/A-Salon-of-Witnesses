# A-Salon-of-Witnesses
A murder mystery that turns out to be an explanation of the transformer, the architecture behind ChatGPT: twelve witnesses at a snowbound manor solve a killing the way attention layers read a sentence, and the reveal waits until the last page.

On the night of the first snow, the clockmaker of Harrow Manor is found dead. Twelve guests each saw one fragment, and no fragment names the killer. A constable interviews the witnesses one at a time and fails, because the two facts that crack the case sit six interviews apart and never meet in his fading notebook. Then Inspector Verity arrives and refuses to interview anyone. She seats all twelve in a circle and gives them one rule: listen to everyone, lean toward whoever touches the thing you yourself saw, and rewrite your own statement. Three rings of her bell later, the room can name a murderer that no single witness ever knew.

The story is a working model of the transformer architecture introduced in the 2017 paper "Attention Is All You Need." The failed constable is a recurrent network. The salon rule is self-attention, the rounds are layers, the guests' several ears are attention heads, the pinned cards are positional encodings, and Verity's ten thousand solved cases are training data. A closing appendix, written so a high school student can follow it, drops the costumes: it maps every story element to its technical counterpart, walks through how the same machinery answers "Why is the sky blue?" one word at a time, and explains why learning to guess the next word forced these machines to learn grammar, facts, and something that behaves a great deal like reasoning. The page is a single self-contained HTML file with no dependencies, styled as a cinematic night scene that dawns into a plain technical appendix at the moment of the reveal.
